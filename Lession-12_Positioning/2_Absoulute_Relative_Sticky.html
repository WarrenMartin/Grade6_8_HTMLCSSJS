CSS Positioning with Sticky Navigation

Relative Positioning

Elements with position: relative move relative to their original position on the page. You can adjust their position without affecting other elements.

Relative Position

When you apply position: relative to an element in CSS, you're not directly telling the browser how to adjust the position relative to the normal document flow. Instead, you're specifying that the element should become a positioned element, and you can then use the top, bottom, left, or right properties to control its position relative to where it would be in the normal document flow.


.relative-demo {
    position: relative;
    left: 10px; /* Move 10 pixels to the right */
}

With this CSS, the element with the class "relative-demo" will be shifted 10 pixels to the right, but it won't affect the position of other elements around it.

Absolute Positioning

With position: absolute, elements are treated as entirely detached entities within the document's layout. They can be precisely situated on the page relative to their nearest positioned ancestor or the page's viewport, without causing any disruption to the layout of neighboring elements. This positioning method offers meticulous control over the placement of elements, making it ideal for creating complex and precisely structured layouts.

Sticky Positioning

Elements with position: sticky stick to a specific position on the page as you scroll. They stay in place until you reach that point, making them great for navigation menus.
